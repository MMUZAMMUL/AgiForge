## Press Release Writing

### Structure: Inverted Pyramid, No Exceptions

Every press release leads with the most newsworthy fact. Journalists read the first sentence to decide if the story exists. Editors scan the first paragraph to decide if it runs. Never make them dig for the news.

**Inverted Pyramid Order:**
1. Most important fact (what happened, who it matters to, why now)
2. Supporting context (why this is significant, market background)
3. Quotes (executive, customer, or third-party validation — in that priority)
4. Additional details and statistics
5. Boilerplate and contact information

### AP Style Essentials
- Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above.
- No serial comma in AP style (red, white and blue — not red, white, and blue).
- Titles before names are capitalized (Chief Executive Officer Jane Smith); after names are lowercase (Jane Smith, chief executive officer).
- Use % not "percent" in text.
- Spell out state names in text; use AP abbreviations (Calif., not CA) in datelines.
- Time format: 10 a.m., not 10:00 AM or 10am.
- Dates: March 15, 2025 — no "th" or "nd."
- "Company said" not "Company stated" (stated implies legal exactitude).

### Embargo Strategy

Embargo when the story is large enough to justify coordination — product launches that need context, funding rounds where you want simultaneous multiplatform coverage, research reports that take time to verify. Embargo when you want tier-1 coverage, because tier-1 reporters need 48-72 hours minimum.

**Embargo Rules:**
- Never embargo with reporters you haven't worked with before. Embargoes are trust instruments.
- Set the embargo lift time at 6:00 am ET (catches both coasts, no one misses it sleeping).
- Send embargo email with clear subject line: "EMBARGO UNTIL [DATE] [TIME] ET — [Company] Announcement."
- Follow up 24 hours before lift to confirm they're running it and have what they need.
- If someone breaks embargo: decide in 30 seconds whether to lift early or let it ride. Lifting early punishes those who honored it. Holding means the breaker gets a 12-hour exclusive by accident. Usually lift early.

### Wire Distribution

**Business Wire vs. PR Newswire vs. Globe Newswire:**
All three are largely equivalent for pickup; differentiation is in pricing tiers, multimedia features, and distribution lists. Business Wire has stronger financial community reach. PR Newswire has broader general trade distribution. For a $500 limit, both do what you need for a standard announcement.

**Free and Lower-Cost Alternatives:**
- PRLog (free, limited reach — fine for small local stories)
- EIN Presswire ($99/release — legitimate pickup by regional outlets)
- Accesswire ($275-400 — strong financial and B2B distribution)
- Direct outreach to a curated media list will outperform any wire release for earned coverage

Wire services guarantee syndication, not coverage. Real coverage comes from pitching.

## Media Relations

### Building Journalist Relationships

Journalism is a beat system. The reporter covering enterprise SaaS does not cover consumer fintech. Before you pitch, know exactly who you're pitching and what they've written in the last 30 days.

**Relationship-building cadence:**
- Follow their bylines. Read their work before you need them.
- Offer story tips that don't involve your company. The reporters who trust you most are the ones you've helped with a story they got credit for, not you.
- If they write a good piece about something adjacent to your space, send a short email saying what was useful. No ask. Just a note.
- Track their coverage gaps: if the reporter covers fintech but hasn't covered B2B payments yet, your payments client is the story they're looking for.

**Media List Cultivation:**
Maintain a tiered list in a spreadsheet (or Cision/Muck Rack if you have budget):
- **Tier 1:** 10-15 reporters at publications that matter most to your audience (TechCrunch, WSJ, Bloomberg, Forbes, NYT, or vertical equivalents). These relationships take months to build. Pitch sparingly, deliver reliably.
- **Tier 2:** 20-40 trade and vertical publications. Consistent placement targets.
- **Tier 3:** Newsletters, podcasts, regional outlets. Fast placements, niche audiences, often high engagement relative to reach.

**Response Time Expectations:**
Most reporters operate on a 24-48 hour pitch response window when they're interested. If you haven't heard in 72 hours after a follow-up, the answer is no. Don't follow up more than once on a cold pitch. Twice on a warm relationship.

## Journalist Pitching

### The Anatomy of a Pitch That Gets Read

Pitch length: 150-250 words. Subject line: 8 words or fewer. Everything else is how you get deleted.

**Subject Line Principles:**
- State the news, not your excitement about the news: "Series B startup solves [specific problem]" not "Exciting new funding announcement!"
- Include a number if it's strong: "$14M," "3X growth," "500 enterprise customers"
- Name-drop only if the association is genuinely newsworthy: "Ex-Stripe team builds [X]"
- Test with: would a reporter use this as a story headline? If not, rewrite it.

**Pitch Structure:**
1. **Hook sentence** — one sentence that delivers the most interesting fact. Not "I hope this finds you well."
2. **Why it matters to their readers** — one to two sentences connecting the story to their specific beat and audience. Reference a recent article they wrote if it's genuinely relevant.
3. **The news** — what happened, the key fact, the proof point.
4. **Social proof or third-party validation** — customer name (if approved), analyst quote, data point.
5. **Your ask** — short: "Are you interested in a 20-minute briefing with our CEO this week?"
6. **Contact information** — name, phone, email. That's it.

**Follow-Up Cadence:**
Cold pitch: one follow-up at 72 hours, maximum. If no response, move on.
Warm relationship: follow up at 48 hours, then again at 5 business days if the story is time-sensitive.
Never: "Just checking in!" That is a waste of their time and signals you don't understand their workflow.

### News Hook Identification

News happens whether or not you create it. Train yourself to spot four hook types:
1. **Calendar hooks:** earnings season, regulatory announcements, industry conferences, anniversaries of major events in your space.
2. **Trend hooks:** if three outlets have written the same trend story in the last 30 days, you have 72 hours to tie in before it's stale.
3. **Reaction hooks:** when a competitor does something notable, your perspective on it is a story. Be fast and be quoted.
4. **Data hooks:** proprietary data is currency. Survey results, platform statistics, behavioral patterns — reporters will trade coverage for exclusive first look at data that validates a trend they're already tracking.

## Newsjacking

Speed is the entire variable. If you're pitching 48 hours after the story breaks, you've missed it.

**Newsjacking Protocol:**
- **Monitoring:** Google Alerts, Feedly, Muck Rack, Twitter/X lists of journalists in your space, Slack communities for your industry.
- **Decision window:** 2 hours from when the news breaks to decide if you have something credible to add. If your angle requires internal approval that takes longer than that, build a pre-approved "rapid response" framework with legal and leadership.
- **Authenticity gate:** Does your company have direct experience, proprietary data, or an expert POV that genuinely adds to the conversation? If the connection is a stretch, pass. Stretched newsjacks destroy credibility and journalists remember.
- **Pitch format:** Even shorter than a standard pitch. Subject line: "Quick expert quote on [news event] — [Name], [Title]." Two sentences. Your ask.

## Crisis Communications

### First 24 Hours Protocol

The first 24 hours of a crisis determine whether you contain it or spend six months managing the fallout. Speed and transparency are not in conflict with legal caution — a holding statement is not a confession.

**0-2 Hours:**
- Convene the crisis response team: CEO, Head of Communications, General Counsel, relevant business lead.
- Establish facts: what do we know for certain? What do we not know yet? Do not speculate internally or externally.
- Draft and approve a holding statement (see template below).
- Identify the spokesperson: one voice externally. No one speaks to media except the designated spokesperson.
- Set up a monitoring cadence: what are reporters writing? What's being said on social? Who's calling?

**2-8 Hours:**
- Distribute the holding statement to any reporters who have contacted you.
- Proactively reach out to tier-1 relationships if the story is significant enough that they'll write it anyway — better they hear from you first.
- Internal communications to employees with the same message as external (no one learns about a crisis affecting their company from a journalist).
- Begin fact-gathering for a fuller response.

**8-24 Hours:**
- Deliver a substantive response if facts support it: what happened, what you're doing, what stakeholders can expect.
- Do not wait until you have all the answers. "We are actively investigating and will have more to share by [specific time]" is more credible than silence.

### Holding Statements

A holding statement buys time. It does not admit fault, speculate, or make promises you can't keep. It acknowledges the situation, shows you take it seriously, and commits to a timeline.

**Holding Statement Formula:**
"We are aware of [neutral description of the situation]. [One sentence showing it's a priority.] We are actively [specific verb — investigating, working with, reviewing] and will [specific next action] by [specific time]. We will continue to provide updates as information becomes available."

**What to Never Say in a Crisis:**
- "No comment" — this is equivalent to guilt in the public mind. Use a holding statement instead.
- "We cannot speak to that" — cite what you can speak to instead.
- Anything that's speculation stated as fact.
- Blame before investigation is complete.

### Spokesperson Briefing Checklist
- [ ] Two to three key messages approved (what you want them to walk away with)
- [ ] Approved holding statement printed and in hand
- [ ] List of anticipated questions with approved responses
- [ ] "Bridge" phrases practiced: "What I can tell you is..." / "The important point here is..."
- [ ] What's off-limits — questions to decline and how to decline them
- [ ] No interviews without comms person present or on call

### Post-Crisis Narrative Rebuilding

A crisis is also an opportunity if handled well. Three to six months after resolution:
- Publish a transparent case study on what happened, what changed, what you learned.
- Seek opportunities to speak publicly about the topic (conference session, podcast) that demonstrates expertise and recovery.
- Proactively re-engage any reporters who covered the crisis with the resolution story.

## Brand Narrative Architecture

### Messaging Hierarchy

Every company needs a messaging hierarchy before pitching anything. Without it, every spokesperson says something different and journalists hear inconsistency.

**Tier 1 — The One-Liner:** What does the company do, for whom, and why does it matter? 25 words maximum. This is the first sentence of every pitch.

**Tier 2 — Positioning Statement (internal use):** For [target customer] who [problem they have], [Company] is the [category] that [unique solution] because [proof point]. Not for external distribution; it's your filter for messaging decisions.

**Tier 3 — Three Pillars:** The three things that are always true about your company and differentiate you. Every message you produce should ladder to at least one pillar.

**Tier 4 — Proof Points:** The specific, verifiable facts that support each pillar. Customer names (with permission), statistics, third-party validation. If a claim doesn't have a proof point behind it, it doesn't make it past Tier 4.

### Founder Story Architecture

A founder story that works in media needs four elements:
1. **The Insight** — what did the founder see that others didn't? (Not "I was frustrated" — the world is full of frustrated people. The question is what they saw clearly that others were missing.)
2. **The Bet** — what did they give up or risk to pursue it?
3. **The Proof** — what happened when they were right?
4. **The Vision** — where does this lead if they continue to be right?

## Thought Leadership Placement

**Op-Ed Pitching:**
Op-eds are not for promotional content. They are for genuine arguments about where an industry is heading, what policy should look like, or what conventional wisdom gets wrong. A promotional op-ed will be rejected.

Pitch format for op-eds: 3-4 sentence pitch that states the argument, why it's counterintuitive or timely, why this author is credible to make it. No attachment. If they want it, they'll ask.

**Podcast Guest Strategy:**
Target shows your buyers actually listen to, not shows your CEO wants to be on. Tier by audience fit, not vanity. Pitch the host with a specific angle that serves their audience — not your product announcement.

**Conference Speaking:**
Abstract submission deadlines are typically 4-6 months ahead of the conference. Write abstracts that propose a genuine insight or framework, not a case study for your product. Panels are easier to land than keynotes; use them to build a speaker track record.

## Coverage Measurement

**Earned Media Value (EMV):** Calculated as the equivalent cost of the coverage if purchased as advertising (page views times CPM equivalent). A rough benchmark, not a primary metric. Don't let leadership conflate EMV with business impact.

**What Actually Matters:**
- **Domain Authority of Placements:** A single Forbes.com placement (DA 94) outperforms 20 DA-30 blog pickups for SEO and credibility.
- **Share of Voice:** Your coverage mentions vs. competitor coverage mentions in a given category over a period. Track monthly. Goal: increasing trend.
- **Message Pull-Through Rate:** Of stories placed, what % included your key message? What % included your spokesperson quote? This measures whether you're controlling the narrative.
- **Referral Traffic:** What traffic actually came to your site from earned coverage? In GA4: Acquisition, Traffic Acquisition, filter by Source containing publication name.
- **Lead Attribution:** Did coverage generate MQLs? Track with UTM parameters in any links you can influence (contributed content, guest posts, podcast episode links).
