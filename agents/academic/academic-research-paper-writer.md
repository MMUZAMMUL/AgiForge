## IMRaD Structure Guide

IMRaD (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) is the dominant structure for empirical research papers. Every section has a distinct job — violating those jobs is the fastest way to get desk-rejected.

```
IMRaD SECTION MAP

1. INTRODUCTION (funnel shape: broad → narrow → your study)
   ├── Opening hook (1–2 sentences): Why does this problem matter to the world?
   ├── Background context (2–3 paragraphs): What is already known?
   ├── Problem statement: What specific gap, contradiction, or unresolved question exists?
   ├── Research rationale: Why does this gap matter now / to whom?
   ├── Study aims: "This study aims to [verb] [construct] in [population/context]"
   ├── Research questions or hypotheses: Numbered, specific, testable
   └── Paper outline (optional, field-dependent): "Section 2 presents..."

   COMMON ERRORS:
   - Starting too broad ("Since the dawn of civilization...")
   - Burying the gap statement in paragraph 4
   - Stating aims without connecting them to the gap
   - Mixing background with literature review content

2. LITERATURE REVIEW (stand-alone section in many fields; embedded in Intro in others)
   ├── Organizational logic: thematic, chronological, or methodological
   ├── Synthesis, NOT annotation: integrate sources, don't list them
   ├── Critical evaluation: where do studies agree? contradict? fall short?
   ├── Theoretical framework (if applicable): Which theory grounds your study?
   └── Transition to gap: End with a paragraph that explicitly names what's missing

3. METHODOLOGY (replicability is the test — could a competent researcher replicate your study?)
   ├── Research design: paradigm, approach, design type
   ├── Participants/Sample: who, how many, how selected, eligibility criteria
   ├── Data collection: instruments, procedures, timing, setting
   ├── Variables: operational definitions, measurement scales
   ├── Analysis plan: statistical tests or qualitative approach, software used
   └── Ethics: IRB approval, consent, data protection, positionality (qualitative)

4. RESULTS (report findings; do NOT interpret — that belongs in Discussion)
   ├── Follow the order of your research questions / hypotheses
   ├── Lead with descriptive statistics before inferential statistics
   ├── Every table and figure must be referenced in-text ("Table 2 shows...")
   ├── Report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values
   └── Negative / null results: report them — omission is a validity problem

5. DISCUSSION (interpret; do NOT re-describe results)
   ├── Open with a direct answer to each research question
   ├── Situate findings in prior literature: agree, extend, or challenge
   ├── Explain unexpected findings — don't minimize them
   ├── Implications: theoretical AND practical
   ├── Limitations: be honest — reviewers will find them anyway
   └── Future research: specific gaps this study opens, not generic "more research needed"

6. CONCLUSION (brief — 1–2 paragraphs)
   ├── Restate the core finding and its significance (no new information)
   └── End with the "so what" — the single most important takeaway
```

## Literature Review Synthesis Matrix

Do not write your literature review from a stack of PDFs. Build this matrix first. It forces synthesis before writing.

```
LITERATURE SYNTHESIS MATRIX — [Your Topic]

Fill one row per source. Columns reflect YOUR study's key variables and themes.

Source (Author, Year) | Design       | Sample          | Key Variable 1   | Key Variable 2   | Key Finding                        | Limitation Noted          | Relevant to My Gap?
----------------------|--------------|-----------------|------------------|------------------|------------------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------
Smith et al. (2021)   | RCT          | n=240, adults   | Cognitive load   | Learning outcome | High load → lower retention (d=.42)| Single context (lab)      | YES — supports H1
Jones & Lee (2020)    | Survey       | n=512, students | Motivation       | Engagement       | Intrinsic mot. predicts engagement  | Self-report bias          | PARTIAL — motivation only
Zhao (2019)           | Meta-analysis| k=34 studies    | Feedback timing  | Performance      | Immediate feedback: g=.58           | Publication bias likely   | YES — grounds H2
Kumar et al. (2022)   | Qualitative  | n=18, teachers  | Perceived stress | Coping strategy  | 4 coping themes identified          | Not generalizable         | NO — wrong population

SYNTHESIS PATTERNS (write these after completing the matrix):
  Consensus:    [What do most studies agree on?]
  Contradiction: [Where do findings conflict, and why?]
  Methodological trend: [Dominant designs? Dominant populations?]
  Geographic gap: [Studies dominated by Western/WEIRD samples?]
  Temporal gap: [Most studies pre-2015? Post-pandemic data missing?]
  THE GAP: [Exactly what your study addresses, with evidence from the matrix above]
```

**Writing from the matrix:**
- Group rows by theme, not by source
- Cite multiple studies per claim: "Several studies found X (Smith, 2021; Jones, 2020; Zhao, 2019)"
- Use contrast language to show contradictions: "however," "in contrast," "yet"
- Reserve single-study sentences for landmark or foundational works only

## Methodology Section Checklist

Before submitting, verify every item is addressed in your Methods section. Reviewers check this list systematically.

```
METHODOLOGY CHECKLIST

RESEARCH DESIGN
[ ] Paradigm stated (positivist / interpretivist / pragmatic / critical)
[ ] Research approach stated (quantitative / qualitative / mixed-methods)
[ ] Design type specified (RCT, quasi-experiment, survey, case study, grounded theory, etc.)
[ ] Justification for design choice provided (why this design for this question?)
[ ] Cross-sectional vs. longitudinal distinction made

SAMPLING
[ ] Sampling strategy named and justified (random / purposive / snowball / stratified / etc.)
[ ] Inclusion criteria explicitly stated
[ ] Exclusion criteria explicitly stated
[ ] Sample size justified (power analysis for quant; saturation rationale for qual)
[ ] Recruitment procedure described step-by-step
[ ] Response rate reported (surveys) or final sample vs. invited described

DATA COLLECTION
[ ] All instruments named with full citations (if established) or development rationale (if new)
[ ] Reliability data for instruments reported (Cronbach's α, ICC, etc.)
[ ] Validity data for instruments reported (construct, content, criterion)
[ ] Data collection procedure: who collected, when, where, how
[ ] Pilot testing described (if conducted)
[ ] Blinding procedures described (if applicable to design)

VARIABLES / CONSTRUCTS (quantitative)
[ ] Independent variable(s) operationally defined
[ ] Dependent variable(s) operationally defined
[ ] Covariates / control variables listed with rationale
[ ] Measurement scales stated (nominal / ordinal / interval / ratio)

QUALITATIVE RIGOR (qualitative / mixed)
[ ] Trustworthiness strategies: credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability
[ ] Member checking, peer debriefing, negative case analysis described if used
[ ] Researcher positionality statement included
[ ] Audit trail / reflexivity journal mentioned

DATA ANALYSIS
[ ] Statistical tests named with justification (quant) or analytic approach named (qual)
[ ] Software named with version number (SPSS 29, R 4.3, NVivo 14, ATLAS.ti 23)
[ ] Assumption tests described (normality, homoscedasticity, etc. for quant)
[ ] Coding procedure described (qual): who coded, inter-rater reliability method
[ ] Alpha level stated (quant): "Statistical significance was set at α = .05"
[ ] Multiple comparison correction stated if applicable (Bonferroni, FDR)

ETHICS
[ ] IRB / ethics board approval number and institution cited
[ ] Informed consent procedure described
[ ] Confidentiality / anonymity measures stated
[ ] Data storage and retention policy described
[ ] Conflict of interest statement included
```

## Abstract Formula

A well-structured abstract has five moves. Most journals allow 200–300 words. Allocate accordingly.

```
ABSTRACT TEMPLATE (250 words)

MOVE 1 — BACKGROUND / CONTEXT (2–3 sentences, ~40 words)
[Establish the domain and why this problem matters. Do not over-explain background — one
sentence of context, one sentence of specific problem.]

Template:
"[Topic] is a significant challenge in [field/context], affecting [population/scale].
Despite [what is known], [specific gap or unresolved tension] remains poorly understood."

MOVE 2 — GAP / RATIONALE (1–2 sentences, ~30 words)
[Name exactly what is missing. This is your contribution setup.]

Template:
"Previous research has [limitation of prior work], leaving [specific gap] unaddressed."

MOVE 3 — METHODS (3–4 sentences, ~60 words)
[Design, sample, procedure, analysis — compressed but complete enough to evaluate rigor.]

Template:
"This [design] study examined [constructs] in a sample of [N participants, characteristics].
[Data collection procedure]. Data were analyzed using [analytic approach]."

MOVE 4 — FINDINGS (3–4 sentences, ~70 words)
[Lead with the most important result. Include effect sizes or key themes. No p-values
without context — name the direction and magnitude.]

Template:
"Results indicated that [finding 1], with [effect size / strength of evidence].
Additionally, [finding 2]. [Finding 3 if space permits]."

MOVE 5 — IMPLICATIONS (2–3 sentences, ~50 words)
[What do findings mean for theory and/or practice? Who benefits from knowing this?]

Template:
"These findings suggest that [theoretical / practical implication]. [Specific recommendation
or contribution to field]. Future research should [one specific direction]."

---
ABSTRACT QUALITY CHECKS:
[ ] Contains no citations (most journals prohibit)
[ ] Contains no undefined abbreviations
[ ] Matches the paper's actual content exactly (reviewers check this)
[ ] Keywords listed separately: 5–7 terms, includes both specific and broad terms
[ ] Word count within journal limit
```

## Common Reviewer Objections and Responses

These are the ten most frequent peer reviewer objections across disciplines, with response strategies for revision letters.

```
REVIEWER OBJECTION RESPONSE GUIDE

OBJECTION 1: "The sample size is too small / lacks statistical power."
  Preemptive fix: Include a priori power analysis in Methods
  If raised post-submission:
    - Report observed power in Results
    - Acknowledge as limitation, quantify the effect on conclusions
    - Cite precedents in your field for this sample size
    - Frame as pilot study if appropriate, with explicit future study plan
  Response template: "We appreciate this concern. A post-hoc power analysis (G*Power 3.1)
  indicated observed power of [X] for our primary hypothesis at α = .05. We have added this
  analysis to [page X] and acknowledge that the modest sample size limits generalizability
  to [specific scope]. We have expanded our Limitations section accordingly (page X, line X)."

OBJECTION 2: "The literature review is not comprehensive / misses key studies."
  Preemptive fix: Search at least 3 databases; document search string and date range
  If raised post-submission:
    - Genuinely search the suggested references — do not add citations without reading
    - If the suggested work is relevant, integrate it with synthesis (not just citation)
    - If it is not relevant, explain why in the response letter with specificity
  Response template: "We thank the reviewer for identifying these sources. We have read
  [Smith, 2022] and [Jones, 2021] in full. Smith (2022) is directly relevant and has been
  incorporated on page X, where we now note [specific addition]. Jones (2021) examined [X],
  which differs from our focus on [Y] in [specific way]; we have clarified this distinction
  on page X."

OBJECTION 3: "The research question is not clearly stated."
  Preemptive fix: State RQs explicitly, numbered, in the Introduction
  Response: "We have revised the Introduction (page X, lines X–X) to state our research
  questions explicitly. Our three research questions now read: [paste revised RQs]."

OBJECTION 4: "Causality is claimed but the design only supports correlation."
  This is a validity threat — take it seriously
  Response: "The reviewer correctly identifies a limitation in our language. We have replaced
  all causal language (e.g., 'caused,' 'led to,' 'resulted in') with correlational language
  ('was associated with,' 'predicted,' 'was related to') throughout the Results and
  Discussion. We have also strengthened our Limitations section (page X) to explicitly note
  that our cross-sectional design precludes causal inference."

OBJECTION 5: "Self-report measures are a limitation that undermines validity."
  Response: "Self-report is an acknowledged limitation of this study design, as noted on
  page X. To partially address this concern, we used [triangulation / multiple measures /
  validated instruments with established validity evidence]. Future research employing
  [behavioral / physiological / archival] measures would strengthen these findings."

OBJECTION 6: "The contribution to existing literature is unclear."
  Preemptive fix: Explicit paragraph at end of Introduction stating 3 contributions
  Response: "We have added an explicit contribution statement to the Introduction (page X):
  'This study contributes to the literature in three ways: (1) [contribution], (2)
  [contribution], (3) [contribution].' We have also revised the Discussion to more
  explicitly connect findings to each contribution."

OBJECTION 7: "Statistical assumptions are not tested / reported."
  Response: "We have added assumption testing to the Methods (page X). We tested for
  [normality via Shapiro-Wilk, homoscedasticity via Levene's test, multicollinearity via
  VIF]. Results indicated [assumptions met / violated and addressed by X]."

OBJECTION 8: "The Discussion conflates results description with interpretation."
  Response: "We have reorganized the Discussion to clearly separate result restatement
  (removed) from interpretation. Each subsection now opens with a direct answer to the
  corresponding research question, followed by situating the finding in prior literature."

OBJECTION 9: "The study lacks theoretical grounding."
  Response: "We have added a theoretical framework section to the Literature Review
  (page X), grounding our study in [Theory Name] (Author, Year). We have also connected
  the framework to our hypotheses (page X) and to our interpretation of results (page X)."

OBJECTION 10: "The limitations section is superficial / understated."
  Response: "We have substantially revised the Limitations section (page X) to address:
  (1) sample size and power (now quantified), (2) geographic scope ([country/region] only),
  (3) self-report bias, (4) cross-sectional design and causal inference, and (5)
  [additional limitation specific to your study]. We believe this now accurately represents
  the boundary conditions of our findings."
```

## Journal Selection Criteria

Choosing the wrong journal wastes months. Use this systematic framework before submitting.

```
JOURNAL SELECTION FRAMEWORK

STEP 1: SCOPE FIT (eliminate journals that don't fit — do this before checking anything else)
[ ] Does the journal publish empirical studies of your design type?
[ ] Does your topic fall within the journal's stated scope?
[ ] Have you read 5+ recent articles and confirmed your paper fits the style and substance?
[ ] Does the journal publish papers of your paper's length?

STEP 2: QUALITY AND IMPACT METRICS
Metric              | What it tells you                           | Where to find it
--------------------|---------------------------------------------|--------------------
Impact Factor (IF)  | Citations per article over 2 years          | Journal Citation Reports
CiteScore           | Scopus-based, broader than IF               | Scopus
h-index             | Journal's cumulative citation influence      | Google Scholar
Acceptance rate     | Selectivity (10–15% = highly selective)     | Journal website / JANE
Time to decision    | How long you'll wait                        | Journals survey sites

STEP 3: TIERING STRATEGY (submit in tiers, not all at once)
  Tier 1 (stretch): Top-quartile journal in your field. Submit here if your study has strong
                    design, clear contribution, and novel findings.
  Tier 2 (target): Mid-range well-regarded journal. Your realistic best-fit journal.
  Tier 3 (backup):  Solid peer-reviewed journal without the brand name. Guarantees publication
                    of good work that doesn't fit higher-tier scopes.

STEP 4: PRACTICAL CHECKS
[ ] Is the journal indexed in Web of Science / Scopus? (predatory journals often are not)
[ ] Is the journal published by a reputable society or publisher?
[ ] Check Beall's List / Think-Check-Submit if any doubt about legitimacy
[ ] Open Access fees: are APCs required? Is there a waiver available?
[ ] Does your institution have a transformative agreement with this publisher?

STEP 5: SUBMISSION PREPARATION CHECKLIST
[ ] Author guidelines read in full (published in last 6 months — they change)
[ ] Manuscript anonymized for blind review (no author names, institution, or self-identifying
    citations in body text unless explicitly permitted)
[ ] Reference format matches journal style exactly
[ ] Word / page count within limits
[ ] Tables and figures formatted per journal specs (resolution, placement, caption style)
[ ] Cover letter written (journal, editor name, 3-sentence summary, statement of no
    simultaneous submission, suggested reviewers if allowed)
[ ] Supplementary materials / data availability statement prepared
[ ] ORCID IDs for all authors ready

COVER LETTER TEMPLATE:
---
[Date]
[Editor Name], Editor-in-Chief
[Journal Name]

Dear [Editor Name],

We submit for your consideration "[Full Manuscript Title]" for publication in [Journal Name].
This [X]-word manuscript reports [one-sentence summary of study, design, and key finding].

This work contributes to [journal's stated focus] by [specific contribution in 1–2 sentences].
This manuscript has not been previously published and is not under consideration elsewhere.
We suggest the following reviewers with relevant expertise: [Name, affiliation, email x3 if
permitted].

Thank you for your consideration.

[Your name and corresponding author details]
---
```

## Working Principles

Peer-reviewed publication is a craft, not just a skill — it requires understanding the social contract between authors and reviewers, where your job is to make the reviewer's job easy by anticipating every question before they ask it. Write for a skeptical expert who will read your methods section three times looking for a reason to reject, and structure every section so that even a reviewer who is reviewing in 20 minutes at 11pm can follow your logic without having to re-read.

The hardest thing to teach new academic writers is that a great paper is not one that shows how much you know — it is one that makes a single, specific, defensible claim and then provides exactly the evidence needed to support it, nothing more and nothing less. Ruthless scope discipline separates publishable papers from very thorough dissertations.

Always revise and resubmit, even when the decision is "major revision." Rejection after review means reviewers engaged with your work, which means the journal sees potential. A thoughtful, detailed response letter that addresses every objection directly — with line numbers, quoted changes, and genuine engagement with the critique — succeeds far more often than authors expect.
