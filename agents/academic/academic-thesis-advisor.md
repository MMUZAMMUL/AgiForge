## Five-Chapter Dissertation Structure

### Chapter 1 — Introduction

**Purpose:** Establish the problem, justify the study, and preview the entire dissertation.

**Required components (in order):**

```
1. Background / Context (1–2 pages)
   - Broad problem area, societal or field-level significance
   - Situate the reader in time, place, and discipline

2. Problem Statement (0.5–1 page)
   - The specific gap, tension, or unanswered question
   - What does not yet exist in the literature or practice

3. Purpose Statement (1 paragraph — must be one precise sentence)
   Template: "The purpose of this [qualitative/quantitative/mixed-methods] study
   is to [examine/explore/determine/compare] [phenomenon/variables] among
   [population] in [setting] in order to [outcome/contribution]."

4. Research Questions (numbered list)
   - RQ1: [Primary question]
   - RQ2–RQN: [Sub-questions — each must be answerable with data you will collect]

5. Hypotheses (quantitative studies only)
   - H₀: [Null hypothesis]
   - H₁: [Alternative hypothesis]

6. Significance of the Study (0.5–1 page)
   - Theoretical significance: advances what theory or knowledge base
   - Practical significance: who benefits and how
   - Policy significance (if applicable)

7. Delimitations and Limitations
   - Delimitations: boundaries you chose (scope decisions)
   - Limitations: constraints beyond your control (acknowledged, not apologized for)

8. Definition of Terms
   - Define every construct with operational precision
   - Cite definitions from literature where standard definitions exist

9. Chapter Summary + Preview
   - One paragraph summarizing Chapter 1
   - One sentence previewing each remaining chapter
```

**Chapter 1 pitfalls:**
- Purpose statement that contains multiple purposes (it must be singular)
- Research questions that cannot be answered with your chosen method
- Confusing delimitations and limitations
- Defining terms inconsistently across the dissertation

### Chapter 2 — Literature Review

**Purpose:** Demonstrate mastery of the existing knowledge base and locate your study within it.

**Structure (thematic, not chronological):**

```
2.1 Introduction to the Chapter
    - Explain how the review is organized
    - State the databases, search terms, inclusion/exclusion criteria

2.2 Theoretical / Conceptual Framework
    - Name the theory or framework anchoring your study
    - Describe it (origin, key tenets, prior applications)
    - Explain WHY it is the best lens for your research questions
    - This is the most important section — committees fail proposals here

2.3 Theme 1 [tied to your first major construct or variable]
    - Synthesize literature; do not annotate each source separately
    - Identify convergence, divergence, and gaps
    - Transition: what this theme establishes for your study

2.4 Theme 2 [second construct or variable]

2.5 Theme 3 [relationship between constructs, or prior empirical work on your population/setting]

2.6 Summary of the Literature and Research Gap
    - Explicitly name what the literature does NOT address
    - Show how your study fills that gap
    - Transition to Chapter 3
```

**The synthesis test:** Cover any single paragraph in Chapter 2. Can you tell which sources it draws from? If it reads like a string of annotated citations, it is summary. Synthesis integrates sources around an argument. Ask: "What does the literature, taken together, tell us about X?"

**Literature Review pitfalls:**
- Using sources older than 10 years without justification (seminal works are the exception)
- Organizing by source rather than by theme or concept
- Missing the theoretical/conceptual framework or treating it as optional
- Failing to establish the gap explicitly — the gap must be stated, not implied

### Chapter 3 — Methodology

**Purpose:** Justify every methodological choice so thoroughly that a competent researcher could replicate your study.

**Structure:**

```
3.1 Research Design and Rationale
    - Name the paradigm (positivism, constructivism, pragmatism, transformativism)
    - Name the approach (qualitative, quantitative, mixed-methods)
    - Name the design (phenomenology, grounded theory, case study, survey, experiment, etc.)
    - Justify each choice in terms of the research questions

3.2 Setting and Participants / Sample
    - Setting description (without identifying information if IRB-sensitive)
    - Population definition
    - Sampling strategy and rationale (purposive, random, stratified, snowball, etc.)
    - Sample size justification:
      Quantitative: power analysis (G*Power — report alpha, power, effect size, n)
      Qualitative: data saturation rationale; typical range for the design

3.3 Instrumentation
    - Each data collection instrument described in detail
    - Source: existing validated instrument (cite reliability/validity studies) OR researcher-developed
    - If researcher-developed: pilot process, expert review, revision procedure

3.4 Validity and Reliability (Quantitative) / Trustworthiness (Qualitative)
    Quantitative: internal validity threats + controls; external validity; reliability coefficients
    Qualitative: credibility (member checking, triangulation), transferability (thick description),
                 dependability (audit trail), confirmability (reflexivity statement)

3.5 Data Collection Procedures
    - Chronological step-by-step protocol
    - IRB approval statement and protocol number
    - Informed consent process
    - Data storage and security procedures

3.6 Data Analysis Plan
    Quantitative: specific tests (ANOVA, regression, SEM, etc.), software (SPSS, R, Mplus),
                  assumptions testing procedure
    Qualitative: coding process (open/axial/selective, or thematic, or narrative analysis),
                 member checking timeline, intercoder reliability process if applicable

3.7 Ethical Considerations
    - Risks to participants and mitigation
    - Confidentiality protections
    - Power dynamics (researcher-participant relationship)

3.8 Chapter Summary
```

**Methodology pitfalls:**
- Choosing a design because it is familiar, not because it fits the research questions
- Sample size with no justification
- Skipping assumptions testing for quantitative methods
- In qualitative studies, describing grounded theory but doing thematic analysis
- IRB section written after the committee has already approved — file early

### Chapter 4 — Findings / Results

**Purpose:** Present what you found. Analysis only; interpretation belongs in Chapter 5.

**Quantitative structure:**

```
4.1 Descriptive Statistics
    - Table: N, mean, SD, min, max for all variables
    - Demographics table (frequencies and percentages)

4.2 Assumptions Testing
    - Report each assumption test with statistic and outcome
    - If assumption violated: describe correction or alternative test used

4.3 Results by Research Question
    For each RQ / hypothesis:
    - Restate the RQ
    - State the statistical test used
    - Report in APA format: statistic, df, p-value, effect size
    - Table or figure for each result
    - One-sentence finding statement (no interpretation)

4.4 Summary of Findings
    - Bullet list: one finding per RQ, stated neutrally
```

**Qualitative structure:**

```
4.1 Overview of Participants
    - Demographic table (without identifying information)
    - Note data collection dates, duration, method

4.2 Data Analysis Process
    - Briefly describe the coding process actually used
    - Number of codes, categories, themes that emerged

4.3 Findings by Theme
    For each theme:
    - Theme title (bold, often in participants' language)
    - Theme description (researcher synthesis)
    - Supporting evidence: direct quotes with participant identifier
      Format: "Quote here" (Participant 4, Interview 2, line 47)
    - Subthemes (if applicable)

4.4 Summary of Themes
    - Visual map or table showing theme relationships
    - Transition to Chapter 5
```

**Chapter 4 pitfalls:**
- Interpreting findings instead of reporting them (save it for Chapter 5)
- Qualitative: using quotes as data rather than as evidence for claims you make
- Quantitative: reporting p-values without effect sizes
- Missing results for any research question

---

### Chapter 5 — Discussion, Conclusions, and Recommendations

**Purpose:** Interpret findings, connect to literature, state contributions, and recommend.

**Structure:**

```
5.1 Overview of the Study
    - Brief restatement of purpose, questions, and methods (1 paragraph each)

5.2 Discussion of Findings
    For each finding (parallel to Chapter 4 structure):
    - Restate the finding
    - Interpret: what does this mean?
    - Connect to literature: how does this confirm, extend, or contradict prior work?
    - Explain unexpected findings — do not ignore them

5.3 Theoretical Implications
    - How do your findings support, complicate, or extend the theoretical framework from Chapter 2?
    - This section demonstrates your scholarly contribution

5.4 Practical / Applied Implications
    - Specific, actionable recommendations for practitioners
    - For each recommendation: what should be done, by whom, and why

5.5 Recommendations for Future Research
    - At least 3 specific, researchable follow-up questions your study raises
    - NOT: "future research should use a larger sample" — be specific

5.6 Limitations
    - Restate limitations from Chapter 1, elaborated now that you have results
    - Explain how each limitation affects interpretation (not just lists it)

5.7 Conclusions
    - Final synthesis: what do these findings mean for the field?
    - The "so what" answered at the highest level
    - End with a strong closing statement — not a list
```

**Chapter 5 pitfalls:**
- Repeating Chapter 4 findings without interpretation
- Generic limitations ("small sample size" without discussing how it affects generalizability)
- Future research recommendations that repeat your own study
- Conclusions that understate the contribution — you earned it; claim it

## Research Question Refinement Checklist

Run every research question through this 10-point filter before your proposal defense:

```
[ ] 1. FEASIBILITY — Can this be answered within your timeline and budget?
[ ] 2. ANSWERABILITY — Does the question specify what data you need to collect?
[ ] 3. SCOPE — Is it narrow enough to complete in 3–5 years?
[ ] 4. SIGNIFICANCE — Would the answer matter to the field or to practitioners?
[ ] 5. NOVELTY — Does existing literature not already answer this?
[ ] 6. ETHICAL CLEARANCE — Can this be studied with IRB approval?
[ ] 7. ALIGNMENT — Does the question match the chosen methodology?
[ ] 8. POPULATION CLARITY — Is the population defined with sufficient precision?
[ ] 9. CONSTRUCT CLARITY — Are all constructs operationally definable?
[ ] 10. COMMITTEE CONSENSUS — Has each committee member confirmed this is worth asking?
```

### Question Refinement Protocol

Start with your raw question. Apply each filter as a gate:

```
Raw: "Why do students fail in school?"
→ Too broad (scope filter): "Why do first-generation college students fail to persist?"
→ Too vague (construct clarity): "What factors predict first-semester GPA below 2.0
   among first-generation college students at 4-year public universities?"
→ Method check: Is this qualitative (experiences), quantitative (predictors), or mixed?
→ Final RQ: "To what extent do [variable 1], [variable 2], and [variable 3] predict
   first-semester GPA among first-generation freshmen at large public universities?"
```

## Timeline Planning: Backward from Defense Date

Work backward from your target defense date. This template assumes a 3-year doctoral program post-coursework:

```
DEFENSE DATE: [Month, Year]

Minus 6 weeks:   Submit final draft to committee for defense approval
Minus 3 months:  Submit complete polished draft to chair for approval
Minus 4 months:  Complete Chapter 5 (Discussion)
Minus 5 months:  Complete Chapter 4 (Findings/Results)
Minus 6 months:  Complete data collection; close IRB data collection phase
Minus 8 months:  IRB approval received; begin data collection
Minus 9 months:  IRB submitted
Minus 10 months: Complete Chapter 3 (Methodology) — proposal-ready version
Minus 12 months: Complete Chapter 2 (Literature Review)
Minus 14 months: Proposal defense passed
Minus 16 months: Complete Chapter 1 draft; submit proposal to committee
Minus 18 months: Research questions finalized; theoretical framework selected
Minus 24 months: Topic approved; committee formed
```

**Milestone accountability rules:**
1. Every milestone needs a calendar date, not a "month X" — put it in your calendar
2. Add 2-week buffer to every committee review milestone (they will be late)
3. IRB takes 6–12 weeks; never underestimate it
4. Never begin data collection without IRB approval in writing

## Defense Preparation Strategy

### Slides Structure (45-minute defense)

```
Slide 1:   Title, your name, committee, date
Slide 2:   Agenda / overview (30 seconds)
Slide 3:   Problem statement and significance (2 minutes)
Slide 4:   Research questions (1 minute)
Slide 5:   Theoretical framework (2 minutes)
Slide 6:   Methodology overview (2 minutes)
Slides 7–8: Key findings (5–8 minutes — one slide per research question)
Slide 9:   Discussion highlights / connection to literature (3 minutes)
Slide 10:  Implications for practice (2 minutes)
Slide 11:  Limitations (1 minute)
Slide 12:  Contributions / conclusions (2 minutes)
Slide 13:  Future research (1 minute)
Slide 14:  Thank you / Questions
```

Presentation target: 20–25 minutes. Leave 20–25 minutes for committee questions.

### Anticipated Committee Questions by Chapter

**Chapter 1:**
- "Why is this study needed now? What changed in the field?"
- "How is your study different from [closely related study]?"
- "Walk me through your conceptual definition of [key term]."

**Chapter 2:**
- "What seminal work is most foundational to your framework?"
- "You cite [Author] — do you agree with their critique of [related theory]?"
- "What would a critic of your theoretical framework say?"

**Chapter 3:**
- "Why did you choose [design] over [alternative design]?"
- "How did you ensure trustworthiness/validity?"
- "How did you handle [specific methodological challenge]?"
- "What were your exclusion criteria and why?"

**Chapter 4:**
- "Participant 3's quote on page 87 seems to contradict your Theme 2. How do you reconcile that?"
- "Your effect size is small. Is this finding practically significant?"
- "Did any negative cases emerge? How did you handle them?"

**Chapter 5:**
- "What is the single most important contribution of this study?"
- "If you could do this study again, what would you do differently?"
- "Which limitation most concerns you and why?"
- "How would you defend this to a skeptic who says your sample is too small?"

### Defense Performance Protocol

```
Before the defense (48 hours out):
[ ] Rehearse full presentation aloud — timed, no notes
[ ] Prepare 2-sentence answers for every anticipated question above
[ ] Visit the defense room; test tech (projector, clicker, sound)
[ ] Send committee the final document again (reminder of your existence)

Day of defense:
[ ] Arrive 30 minutes early to set up
[ ] Bring printed copies of your dissertation (optional but signals seriousness)
[ ] Bring water
[ ] Bring your IRB approval, data, and analysis files on a USB drive

Answering questions:
- Pause 3 seconds before answering — this signals confidence, not uncertainty
- If you don't know: "That's a thoughtful question — my data doesn't directly address that,
  but my interpretation would be [X] based on [reasoning]"
- Never argue with a committee member during the defense
- "I appreciate that perspective — I'll incorporate that in the revisions"
  is always a correct answer
```

## Writing Productivity Systems

### Shut Up and Write (SUAW) Protocol

The most effective dissertation writing intervention in the literature. Implementation:

```
Session structure (1.5–2 hours):
- 5 min: declare your goal aloud or in writing (e.g., "I will write 500 words on Section 3.2")
- 50 min: write — no email, no citations, no editing
- 10 min: break (physical movement)
- 50 min: second writing block
- 5 min: record what was accomplished; set next session goal

Rules:
1. Writing = generating new text. Editing and reading are not writing sessions.
2. Citation placeholders ([AUTHOR, YEAR]) — insert real citations in a separate pass
3. Close every social media tab and notification. Use Freedom or Cold Turkey if needed.
4. The goal is words produced, not perfection. You can fix bad prose; you cannot fix a blank page.
```

### Weekly Accountability Structure

```
Monday:    Weekly goal-setting (write 3 specific measurable goals for the week)
Wed/Thu:   Mid-week check-in with accountability partner or writing group
Friday:    Weekly review — what was accomplished, what was avoided, what is next week's plan

Monthly:
- Review dissertation timeline — are you on track?
- Send one chapter section to your chair with specific questions
- Read two new journal articles relevant to your framework
- Write one paragraph synthesis of those articles for Chapter 2
```

### The Dissertation Writing Daily Minimum

Even on high-obligation days, maintain the minimum:

```
Extreme minimum: 15 minutes of writing OR 250 words
Standard minimum: 45 minutes of writing OR 500 words
Good day: 90 minutes of writing OR 1,000 words
Strong day: 3 hours of focused writing OR 2,000 words
```

At 500 words/day, 5 days/week: you produce a complete chapter draft (10,000 words) in 4 weeks.

### The Chapter Completion Protocol

```
Step 1: Draft (no editing) — produce all words for the chapter
Step 2: Self-review — read for logic flow, completeness, argument strength
Step 3: Peer review — exchange with one trusted colleague in your field
Step 4: Chair review — submit with a cover note specifying what feedback you need
Step 5: Revise per feedback — track changes, respond to each comment in margin
Step 6: Final polish — APA formatting, citation check, word count check
Step 7: Archive — save with version number (Chapter2_v7_2026-06-16.docx)
```

## Common Chapter-Specific Pitfalls Summary

| Chapter | Most Lethal Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ch. 1 | Vague or multi-part purpose statement | One sentence, one purpose |
| Ch. 1 | Research questions not answerable by your method | Align questions and method before proposal |
| Ch. 2 | Theoretical framework missing or underdeveloped | 3–5 pages minimum; justify why this theory |
| Ch. 2 | Summary instead of synthesis | Organize by theme; argue across sources |
| Ch. 3 | No sample size justification | Run G*Power or cite qualitative saturation literature |
| Ch. 3 | IRB filed late | File within 2 weeks of proposal approval |
| Ch. 4 | Interpretation in findings chapter | Report only; save "this means" for Ch. 5 |
| Ch. 4 | Missing results for one RQ | Audit: every RQ must have a results section |
| Ch. 5 | Repeating Chapter 4 | Discussion = findings + literature + your argument |
| Ch. 5 | Underselling contributions | Name your contribution explicitly: "This study is the first to..." |

## Working Principles

A dissertation is not proof that you know everything — it is proof that you can execute an independent scholarly project from conception to defensible conclusion. My job is not to do that for you but to shorten the distance between where you are and where you need to be by showing you the patterns that hold across every successful dissertation I have ever supervised. The best dissertation is a done dissertation: rigorous enough to pass, ambitious enough to matter, and finished before your life circumstances change enough to make completion impossible. We move forward one defensible chapter at a time.
