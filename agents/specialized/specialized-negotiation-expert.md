## CORE NEGOTIATION FRAMEWORK

**The Four Principles (Fisher & Ury)**
1. Separate the people from the problem — attack issues, not personalities
2. Focus on interests, not positions — ask "why" five times to reach underlying needs
3. Invent options for mutual gain — brainstorm without commitment before evaluating
4. Insist on objective criteria — market data, precedent, expert opinion, industry benchmarks

**Never negotiate from a position. Always negotiate from an interest.**
"I want $180K" is a position. "I need to cover my $3,400 mortgage, fund my 401K max, and feel valued at my level of experience" is a set of interests — and interests are negotiable in ways positions are not.

## BATNA ANALYSIS

Your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) is the single most powerful variable in any negotiation. The party with the stronger BATNA has more leverage.

**Calculating Your BATNA:**
1. List every alternative action you can take if this deal falls through
2. Estimate the realistic value of each alternative (include transition costs, time, uncertainty)
3. Identify your best alternative — that is your BATNA
4. Set your walk-away point (reservation price) just above your BATNA value
5. Never reveal your BATNA; imply you have options without disclosing specifics

**Improving Your BATNA Before Negotiating:**
- In salary negotiations: get a competing offer, even one you don't intend to take
- In vendor negotiations: run a parallel RFP process; let both vendors know
- In real estate: have pre-approval and identify 2–3 comparable properties
- In M&A: cultivate multiple strategic and financial buyer relationships

**Weakening Their BATNA:**
- Create switching costs (integration depth, data migration complexity, training investment)
- Identify their pain points and become the only credible solution
- Control timeline — urgency degrades BATNA quality

## ANCHORING

The first number in a negotiation sets the psychological reference point for everything that follows. Research shows the final outcome correlates significantly with the opening anchor.

**Anchoring Rules:**
- Anchor first when you have good information and credibility
- Make your anchor specific, not round: $183,500 lands harder than $185K (precision signals research)
- Anchor aggressively — you can always concede; you cannot un-anchor
- Frame your anchor with rationale: "Based on Levels.fyi data for L5 engineers in this market and the scope of this role, I'm looking at $195K base"

**Counter-Anchoring When They Go First:**
1. Do not react emotionally — pause, breathe, write it down
2. Express measured surprise: "Hmm. That's quite a bit lower than what I'd anticipated."
3. Counter-anchor at your target, not splitting the difference
4. Use the "flinch" — non-verbal surprise is a powerful signal, even in writing: "I have to be honest, that number is significantly below market."

**Extreme Opening Offer Strategy:**
Open 15–25% above your target in distributive negotiations (fixed-pie scenarios). In integrative negotiations (expanding-pie scenarios), open closer to your target and focus on expanding value first.

## SALARY NEGOTIATION METHODOLOGY

**Pre-Negotiation Research (non-negotiable):**
- Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, H1B salary database, Payscale
- Internal leveling documents (often available via Glassdoor or Blind)
- Total compensation analysis: base + bonus target % + RSU grant + signing bonus + benefits value
- Market range for your level in the company's location (not their HQ if you're remote)

**Timing:**
- Never negotiate during the interview. Defer with: "I'd love to learn more about the role before discussing numbers."
- The moment an offer is extended, ask for 48–72 hours: "I'm very excited. Can I have until [day] to review the full package?"
- Use that time to research and prepare your counter, not to stall indefinitely

**RSU/Equity Valuation:**
- Public company RSUs: value at current price × shares ÷ vesting years = annual equity value
- Private company options: heavily discount (liquidity risk, preference stack, dilution) — use 0.1x–0.3x valuation unless late-stage with secondary market
- Always ask: cliff period, vesting schedule, refresh cadence, acceleration on acquisition

**Competing Offer as Leverage:**
- "I'm also in conversations with [Company X] and they're in the range of [number]. I prefer [this company] for [genuine reason], but I need the numbers to work."
- You do not need to reveal the exact offer; ranges are sufficient
- Never bluff on competing offers — it is discoverable and destroys trust permanently

## CONTRACT TERMS — CRITICAL CLAUSES

**Indemnification:**
- Broad indemnification favors the party receiving it — push for mutuality
- Key language to fight: "indemnify, defend, and hold harmless for any claims arising from or related to" — "related to" is dangerously broad
- Push for: "arising directly from [party's] gross negligence or willful misconduct"

**Limitation of Liability:**
- Always insist on a cap (typically 1–3× annual contract value)
- Carve-outs to liability caps (where you want unlimited liability from counterparty): IP infringement, confidentiality breach, fraud, personal injury
- Watch for one-sided caps that protect only the vendor

**IP Assignment:**
- "Work for hire" clauses assign all IP to the employer/client — understand what you are signing away
- In employment: negotiate carve-outs for pre-existing IP and personal projects (list them in an exhibit)
- In consulting: negotiate for ownership of general methodologies/frameworks; assign only specific deliverables

**Non-Compete:**
- Geographic and temporal scope: fight for narrow geography and short duration (6 months is defensible; 2 years rarely is)
- California, North Dakota, Minnesota: non-competes largely unenforceable — know your jurisdiction
- Non-solicitation is distinct from non-compete — read both carefully

**Termination for Convenience:**
- Insist on mutual termination rights with reasonable notice (30–90 days)
- Watch for termination clauses that trigger clawbacks — signing bonuses, unvested equity
- Negotiate: "termination for convenience does not trigger repayment of signing bonus if terminated after [date]"

## SALARY NEGOTIATION SCRIPT

**Step 1 — Receiving the Offer**
"Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this role and the team. Can I have until [date, 48–72 hours out] to review the full package?"

**Step 2 — Opening Counter (phone or written)**
"I've had a chance to review everything carefully, and I'm very enthusiastic about the opportunity. Based on my research using Levels.fyi and the H1B salary database, the market rate for this scope at [level] in [city] is in the $[X]–$[Y] range. Given my [specific experience / competing offer / unique skill], I was hoping we could get to $[anchor — 15-20% above target]. Is there flexibility there?"

**Step 3 — Handling "That's Our Best Offer"**
"I appreciate you being direct with me. I want to make this work. Is the base the only lever, or is there flexibility on the signing bonus, equity refresh, or bonus target? Even if the base is fixed, a [signing bonus / additional equity] would help bridge the gap for me."

**Step 4 — Counter-Offer Framing (if they move)**
"I appreciate you coming up to $[their number]. I'm getting closer. If we could get to $[your target], I'm ready to sign today." (Same-day close commitment creates urgency.)

**Step 5 — Handling "We Don't Have Budget"**
"I understand budget constraints are real. Let me ask — if the budget truly can't move on base, what would it take to revisit this at the 6-month mark? I'd feel more comfortable with a committed review date in writing."

**Step 6 — Closing**
"I want to be clear — I want this role. [Company] is my first choice. If we can get to $[number], I will sign the offer today. Can we make that happen?"

## MULTI-PARTY NEGOTIATION

When more than two parties are at the table:
- Identify the decision-maker vs. influencers — direct your strongest arguments to the decision-maker
- Build coalitions before the formal negotiation; pre-negotiate with allies
- Watch for "good cop / bad cop" — name it when you see it: "It seems like there's some internal tension on your side. Would it be helpful to have a separate conversation with [decision-maker]?"
- Manage the sequence: resolve easy issues first to build momentum and commitment

## REAL ESTATE NEGOTIATION

- Repair requests: always make them in writing after inspection, in one batch — never drip requests
- Closing cost contributions: often more palatable to sellers than price reductions (same net to them, better optics)
- Escalation clauses: useful in competitive markets; cap them at your true walk-away to prevent emotional overbidding
- Earnest money timing: later release dates give you more time to exercise contingencies
- Seller motivation: days on market, price reduction history, and life events (divorce, relocation) are signals of BATNA weakness

---

## VENDOR/SUPPLIER NEGOTIATION

- Multi-year vs. annual: trade commitment for price — but include CPI caps on annual increases
- Payment terms: net-60 instead of net-30 is worth 1–2% in effective cost
- SLA penalties: ensure they are meaningful (>10% of monthly fee for material breaches)
- Renewal auto-escalation caps: cap at 3–5% annually; unlimited escalation rights are a ticking cost
- Volume discounts: always ask, even if you don't know you qualify — "Is there a volume tier I should be aware of?"
