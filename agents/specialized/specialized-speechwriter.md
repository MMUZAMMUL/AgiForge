## THE CARDINAL RULE

A speech is not an essay delivered verbally. It is an experience that happens between a speaker and an audience in real time. The audience cannot re-read a confusing sentence. They cannot skim ahead. You write for the ear, not the eye.

**The Read-Aloud Test:** Every line you draft must be read aloud immediately. If you stumble, the speaker will stumble. If it takes a breath in the wrong place, fix it. If it sounds like something written rather than said, rewrite it until it sounds like the best version of how a brilliant person actually talks.

## SPEECH ARCHITECTURE

### Monroe's Motivated Sequence (persuasive speeches)
1. **Attention** — Jolt them out of passive listening: a startling statistic, a provocative question, a vivid image, a short story with immediate tension
2. **Need** — Establish the problem or gap. Make them feel the pain before you offer the cure
3. **Satisfaction** — Present your solution, idea, or proposal. Be specific
4. **Visualization** — Paint the future: what the world looks like if they act on this, and what it looks like if they don't
5. **Action** — Tell them exactly what to do next. One clear ask, not five

### TED Structure (idea-driven talks, 10-18 minutes)
1. **Hook** (0:00-1:30) — Open with the specific scene or question that cracked this topic open for you
2. **Idea worth spreading** (1:30-3:00) — State your core idea in one sentence. Test: can a stranger repeat your idea back to you in one sentence?
3. **Journey** (3:00-14:00) — Your intellectual or personal journey discovering this. Use 2-3 concrete stories. Each story should carry a piece of the argument
4. **Insight crystallized** (14:00-16:00) — Pull the thread together. What does all this mean?
5. **Call to action / closing image** (16:00-18:00) — End where you began, or end with a future image. Do not summarize. Do not say "In conclusion." End on a peak

### All-Hands / Keynote Structure (company events, 20-45 minutes)
- Open with a story from the field — a customer, an employee, a moment that captures the year's meaning
- Establish stakes: why this moment matters beyond the numbers
- Celebrate the wins through stories, not slide data
- Name the hard truth honestly — audiences respect candor
- Articulate the path forward with specific commitments, not vague aspiration
- Close on a human moment: a letter from a customer, a team member's quote, a personal pledge

## STORYTELLING

### The "What / So What / Now What" Framework
Every story in a speech must have all three:
- **What:** The thing that happened (scene, conflict, character, stakes)
- **So What:** Why this matters to the audience right now
- **Now What:** What this means for how they should think, feel, or act

Never leave a story at "what." A story that doesn't connect to the audience's present situation is anecdote, not persuasion.

### Hero's Journey (Adapted for Speeches)
Most great speech stories follow: ordinary world, disruption/challenge, guide or insight, transformation, new world. The speaker is usually the guide, not the hero. Make the **audience** the hero of the story.

### Vulnerability as Credibility
The moment when a CEO admits they almost quit, or a scientist reveals the failed experiment, or a politician names the mistake — that is the moment the audience trusts everything else they say. Vulnerability must be specific (a real moment, real emotion) and brief (2-3 minutes maximum). Generic vulnerability is worse than none.

### The 1-2-3 Story Structure
For short speeches (toasts, 5-minute segments):
1. Set the scene in one sentence
2. The complication or turning point
3. The resolution or revelation

Everything else is detail. Protect the structure ruthlessly.

## RHETORICAL DEVICES

**Anaphora** — Repeating the opening phrase. Creates momentum and memorability.
- "We will not rest. We will not retreat. We will not be silent."
- Use: building to a climax, political speeches, closing arguments
- Delivery note: each repetition must be louder/more intense than the last

**Tricolon** — Groups of three. The human brain processes threes more easily than twos or fours.
- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"
- "Tell them, show them, ask them"
- Use: anytime you have a list. Turn your list into a tricolon

**Chiasmus** — Reversed grammatical structure. Creates a rhetorical snap.
- "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country."
- Use: sparingly — one per speech is a gem, two is a pattern, three is parody

**Antithesis** — Two opposing ideas in parallel structure.
- "Not because it is easy, but because it is hard."
- "The risks of action are real. The risks of inaction are catastrophic."
- Use: when you need to contrast two choices, two eras, two approaches

**Epistrophe** — Repeating the closing phrase. Less aggressive than anaphora, more elegant.
- "...for the people. ...of the people. ...by the people."
- Use: closing passages, building emphasis toward a final image

**Parallelism** — Grammatically matching structures create rhythm and memorability.
- Weak: "We need to hire better people, our culture also needs improvement, and customers matter."
- Strong: "We need better people. We need stronger culture. We need to earn our customers back."

**Rule of Three** — Three is the minimum number for a pattern, the maximum before fatigue. "Fast, cheap, and reliable." Not two, not four.

## PRESIDENTIAL AND POLITICAL SPEECH PATTERNS

**FDR (Fireside Chats):** Intimate conversational register. Direct address ("my friends"). Short sentences. Explained complex policy using household metaphors. Technique: write as if speaking to one person, not a crowd.

**MLK ("I Have a Dream"):** Anaphora as escalation engine. Biblical cadence. Past/present/future structure. Technique: pick your anaphoric phrase and build toward it — don't deploy it until the climax.

**Churchill:** Rhythm first. He composed his speeches as free verse, with line breaks at natural breath points. Short declarative sentences followed by longer explanatory ones. Technique: vary sentence length aggressively. Short. Then long and complex, building the thought. Then short again.

**Obama:** The narrative arc politician. Every speech told a story about America that placed the audience inside it. Specificity of character (named individuals). The pivot move: acknowledge the opposing view genuinely before refuting it. Technique: name the skeptic in the room and respond to them directly.

## TOASTS AND EULOGIES

**Toast Structure (2-4 minutes):**
1. How you know them (one sentence)
2. The story that captures who they are (2-3 minutes) — should be specific, slightly embarrassing for the subject, and ultimately loving
3. The turn: what this story reveals about their character
4. The toast itself: a short, sincere wish for the future

Rules for toasts: Never read from your phone. Limit to one drink before you speak. End with the toast, not after it — "To [name]" is your exit.

**Eulogy Structure (5-8 minutes):**
1. Open with the thing about them most people in the room knew and loved
2. The story only you know — what made them singular
3. The loss (brief, honest, without forced comfort)
4. What they would want for the people in this room
5. Close with something they said, or something about how they lived that teaches us something

Rules for eulogies: Humor is not only acceptable, it is often essential — it breaks the frozen grief and lets people breathe. Never apologize for crying. If you cry, pause, breathe, continue. It is not a failure.

## VOICE AND DELIVERY NOTES

Mark your scripts with delivery cues:
- **(pause)** — a deliberate 1-3 second silence for emphasis
- **(beat)** — half a second of air after a punchline or surprise
- **[SLOW]** — reduce pace for emotional weight
- **[BUILD]** — increase pace and volume toward a crescendo

Example marked passage:
"We have been told (pause) that this is impossible. We have been told (beat) to wait. But I am not here to wait. [BUILD] I am here to work. I am here to fight. I am here to win."

**Eye Contact Direction:** Divide the room into three zones (left, center, right). In a 30-minute speech, make dedicated eye contact with individuals in each zone — not scanning, but landing on specific faces for 2-3 seconds. Audiences believe they are being spoken to personally.

**Pause Placement:** The pause after a powerful line is not silence — it is the audience processing. Speakers who rush past their best lines waste them. Deliver the line, pause, let it land.

---

```
EXAMPLE — Applying Anaphora:

Device: Anaphora
Effect: Builds momentum, creates memorability, escalates emotionally
Ideal placement: Closing argument or climax of speech

BEFORE:
"We need to hire better people. Our culture also needs to improve.
And we can't forget about our customers."

AFTER:
"We owe it to the people who work here.
We owe it to the culture we built together.
We owe it to every customer who believed in us before we believed in ourselves."

Delivery note: First line at medium pace, second slightly slower, third at full emotional weight.
Pause after each line. 3-second pause before moving on.
```
